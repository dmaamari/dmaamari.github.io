Multi-Lingual Signage along the Bronx River

BRONX, NEW YORK, USA

Context

The Bronx is the most dense and ethnically diverse borough of the five New York boroughs. It boasts the freshwater Bronx River, which flows from Westchester county throughout the borough, all the way to the East River. The land on either side of the river, formerly covered by forests, swamps, marshes, and smaller streams, is now comprised of highly developed commercial and residential neighborhoods.
These new developments are not able to absorb enough rainfall and melting snow (beneficial for purifying water). They further pollute the water that passes through them with inorganic materials. This stormwater runoff often goes untreated into the river and causes further pollution and degradation to the natural environment.


Using Data to Argue for a Policy

In order to address the needs of the largely Hispanic population in the Bronx, I created maps, graphics, and analyses using ACS data from Social Explorer, Excel, and ArcGIS to argue for multi-lingual signage along the Bronx River bank.

Methodology

In order to address the needs of the largely Hispanic population in the Bronx, create maps, graphics and analysis using ACS data from Social Explorer, Excel, and ArcGIS to argue for multi-lingual signage along the Bronx river banks. I began by analyzing the percentage of Hispanic or Latino population density in the census block groups of the Bronx, as provided by the 2011-2015 American Census 5 Year Estimates.
The first map shows the high concentration of Hispanic/Latino population on the Bronx River watershed. This highlights the discrepancy in the populations subjected to environmental and possibly even risk disaster.
The second map shows the correlation between the concentrations of Spanish speakers and people of Hispanic/Latino origin, in order to make a case for the need for new signage in the greenery around the river.
The third map highlights the top 10 Spanish speaking tracts in the Bronx, which happen to fall on nearly the beginning and end of the Bronx River in this borough.


The chart furthers validates the need for new signage to accommodate Spanish speakers by showing that 48% of speakers in the Bronx speak Spanish, whereas only 40% speak English!
